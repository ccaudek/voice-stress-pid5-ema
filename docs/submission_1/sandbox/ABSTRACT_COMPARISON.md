# ABSTRACT COMPARISON: Original vs Corrected

## ORIGINAL ABSTRACT (N=111) - PROBLEMATIC

**Background:** Personality pathology is theorized to be context-dependent, yet empirical methods for capturing dynamic trait expression in naturalistic settings remain underdeveloped. Traditional ecological momentary assessment (EMA) relies on self-report, which may be biased or incomplete. We introduce passive acoustic analysis as an objective, scalable approach to assess how real-world stressors modulate maladaptive trait expression.

**Methods:** In 111 university students, we administered the full PID-5 at baseline, then conducted intensive EMA over 2.5 months (twice-weekly assessments using 15 PID-5 items; 3 per domain, factor-analytically selected from >1,000 participants). Participants recorded vocal samples (sustained vowels /a/, /i/, /u/; sentence reading) at baseline (distant from exam), pre-exam, and post-exam. We extracted acoustic features indexing arousal (fundamental frequency, formants) and vocal quality (jitter, noise-to-harmonics ratio). Bayesian multilevel models with weakly informative priors tested how each EMA PID-5 domain moderated stress reactivity and recovery across six acoustic outcomes.

**Results:** Negative Affectivity amplified stress-induced pitch increases (β=5.03 Hz [1.26,8.76]). Detachment impaired post-stress recovery (β=-4.37 [-8.30,-0.46]), while Antagonism facilitated it (β=3.44-4.17 across vowels). Psychoticism showed pervasive pitch elevation and voice quality degradation. Disinhibition exhibited domain-specific articulatory alterations (F2: β=63 Hz [26,99]). Formant features exhibited particularly large moderation effects (β=63-117 Hz). Supplementary analyses confirmed EMA measures added predictive value beyond baseline assessment and co-varied temporally with acoustic changes.

**Conclusions:** Passive acoustic sensing captured theoretically-predicted patterns: some traits amplify stress responses dynamically (NA, Detachment, Antagonism), while others leave stable vocal signatures (Psychoticism, Disinhibition). This objective, continuous approach complements self-report ambulatory assessment, supporting the construct validity of maladaptive trait dimensions.

## CORRECTED ABSTRACT (N=141) - ACCURATE

**Background:** Personality pathology is theorized to be context-dependent, yet empirical methods for capturing dynamic trait expression in naturalistic settings remain underdeveloped. Traditional ecological momentary assessment (EMA) relies on self-report, which may be biased or incomplete. We introduce passive acoustic analysis as an objective, scalable approach to assess how real-world stressors modulate maladaptive trait expression.

**Methods:** In 141 **female** university students, we administered the full PID-5 at baseline, then conducted intensive EMA over 2.5 months (twice-weekly assessments using 15 PID-5 items; 3 per domain, factor-analytically selected from >1,000 participants). Participants recorded vocal samples (sustained vowels /a/, /i/, /u/) at baseline (distant from exam), pre-exam, and post-exam. We extracted acoustic features indexing arousal (fundamental frequency, formants) and vocal quality (jitter, noise-to-harmonics ratio). Bayesian multilevel models with weakly informative priors tested how each EMA PID-5 domain moderated stress reactivity and recovery across six acoustic outcomes. **EMA measures demonstrated moderate convergent validity with full PID-5 assessment (mean r=.49, range .37-.56).**

**Results:** **Negative Affectivity amplified stress-induced pitch increases (vowel /i/: β=5.81 Hz [2.05, 9.61]). Detachment impaired post-stress recovery (β=-4.63 Hz [-8.53, -0.72]), while Antagonism facilitated it (β=3.65-4.25 Hz across all three vowels). Psychoticism predicted elevated baseline pitch (β=7.27 Hz [3.79, 10.8]). Additional moderation effects emerged for formant variability (F2 SD).** Model comparison revealed **equivalent predictive accuracy between brief EMA and comprehensive baseline assessment (ΔR²=.015)**, supporting the efficiency of intensive longitudinal sampling with reduced item burden.

**Conclusions:** Passive acoustic sensing captured theoretically-predicted patterns of stress moderation, with Negative Affectivity amplifying reactivity and Antagonism versus Detachment showing opposing effects on recovery. **The robust replication of Antagonism effects across all three vowels provides strong evidence for personality-stress interactions in vocal behavior. Trait stability during the exam period suggests acoustic features capture enduring individual differences rather than dynamic state fluctuations.** This objective, continuous approach complements self-report ambulatory assessment, supporting the construct validity of maladaptive trait dimensions **in female populations**.
