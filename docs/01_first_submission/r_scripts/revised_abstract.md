## REVISED ABSTRACT

**Background:** Personality pathology is theorized to be context-dependent, yet empirical methods for capturing dynamic trait expression in naturalistic settings remain underdeveloped. Traditional ecological momentary assessment (EMA) relies on self-report, which may be biased or incomplete. We introduce passive acoustic analysis as an objective, scalable approach to assess how real-world stressors modulate maladaptive trait expression.

**Methods:** In 141 university students (expanded from N=111 in pilot analyses), we administered the full PID-5 at baseline, then conducted intensive EMA over 2.5 months (twice-weekly assessments using 15 PID-5 items; 3 per domain, factor-analytically selected from >1,000 participants). Participants recorded vocal samples (sustained vowels /a/, /i/, /u/) at baseline (distant from exam), pre-exam, and post-exam. We extracted acoustic features indexing arousal (fundamental frequency, formants) and vocal quality (jitter, noise-to-harmonics ratio). Bayesian multilevel models with weakly informative priors tested how each EMA PID-5 domain moderated stress reactivity and recovery across six acoustic outcomes.

**Results:** Academic stress increased pitch 3-4 Hz and paradoxically improved voice quality (reduced jitter, enhanced harmonic clarity), consistent with performance arousal. Negative Affectivity amplified stress-induced pitch increases (β=5.81 Hz [2.05, 9.61]), nearly doubling the average effect. Detachment impaired post-stress recovery (β=-4.63 [-8.53, -0.72]), while Antagonism consistently facilitated it across vowels (β=3.65-4.25). Psychoticism moderated articulatory instability under stress (F2 variability: β=-0.28 [-0.55, -0.00]). Negative Affectivity effects extended to formant stability (F2 SD: β=-0.16 [-0.27, -0.04]), indicating motor control disruption. Supplementary analyses confirmed EMA measures achieved equivalent predictive accuracy to comprehensive baseline assessment (ΔR²<0.025) while offering ecological validity, and that within-person emotional lability uniquely predicted articulatory (not prosodic) stress responses.

**Conclusions:** Passive acoustic sensing captured theoretically-predicted patterns in this larger sample: some traits amplify stress responses dynamically (Negative Affectivity) or impair recovery (Detachment), while others facilitate adaptation (Antagonism) or manifest in articulatory control (Psychoticism). Brief EMA (15 items) matched comprehensive questionnaires (220 items) in predictive validity, validating intensive longitudinal assessment. This objective, continuous approach complements self-report ambulatory assessment, supporting the construct validity of maladaptive trait dimensions and offering a scalable method for digital phenotyping of personality pathology.
