Dear Dr. Ringwald,

We thank you and the two reviewers for the constructive and detailed feedback, which has substantially improved the manuscript. We were glad that you and both reviewers saw value in establishing a low-burden, non-self-report index of stress reactivity for studying personality-pathology processes. We have revised the manuscript thoroughly. The most consequential changes are:

1. **Reframing of the central claim.** We now distinguish clearly throughout between the *naturalistic event* (the examination) and the *controlled speech sample* (sustained vowels recorded in the laboratory), and we have toned down the title, abstract, and discussion accordingly, including the terms "objective," "passive sensing," and "naturalistic speech." We position the present study as establishing the marker in controlled speech anchored to a well-defined real-world event—a necessary first step toward later detection in naturalistic speech.
2. **Empirical validation of the stressor.** Using the EMA data, we added a manipulation check showing that the exam elicited the affective signature of acute stress (elevated state negative affect and threat appraisal before the exam).
3. **Analysis of connected speech.** Responding to the request to use the connected-speech material we collected, we added a multivariate analysis of mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs) of the standardized connected-speech sample, together with a cross-vowel convergence analysis. The connected-speech analysis revealed a modest stress-related spectral shift — weaker and more diffusely distributed than the sustained-vowel F0/NNE signal — confirming that the stress signature is not an artifact of the controlled vowel task, while making the signal-quality/ecological-validity trade-off explicit.
4. **Reframing of the EMA-versus-baseline comparison** as a test of *replication* of moderation effects across measurement methods, rather than as evidence of EMA efficiency or of EMA validity; we revised the "Methodological contribution" section and integrated the EMA personality-pathology literature.
5. **Expanded reporting and corrections:** fuller EMA and Bayesian reporting following Trull and Ebner-Priemer, key material moved into the main text, corrected descriptive statistics, bias-free sex/gender language, a new Conclusion, and data/code made available for review.

We respond to each specific point below.

**R1.9.** *Did you assess how stressed participants felt, and the associations between voice-feature changes and self-reported stress?*

Focusing on the anticipatory phase—where the pre-exam voice and EMA assessments are temporally aligned, unlike the post-exam prompt (see R2.21)—we related the person-level change in F0 (pre minus baseline) to the change in EMA negative affect (pre minus baseline; n = 115). **R1.11.** *With the Bayesian analyses, is multiple testing problematic (analogous to alpha inflation)? What is your position and what counter-measures do you take?*

We have added an explicit treatment. For the multivariate connected-speech analysis we used an omnibus test rather than coefficient-wise testing, precisely to avoid coefficient-level multiplicity (Supplement S[X]). `[PLACEHOLDER: page refs]`

**R2.21.** *The pre-exam = stress and post-exam = recovery assumption is plausible but would benefit from knowing whether participants reported perceived stress, anxiety, importance, preparedness, or exam outcome; post-exam responses may depend on performance.*

We agree that post-exam responses may depend partly on exam outcome, and we have therefore added an exploratory analysis linking exam grades to both subjective EMA responses and vocal measures.

The manipulation check reported in response to E2/R1.1 provides direct evidence that the pre-exam timepoint was experienced as stressful and threatening. Regarding the post-exam phase, the EMA data indicated substantial subjective recovery after the exam: negative affect and stress-related appraisals decreased after the exam, consistent with relief once the exam was over and grades had been communicated. In contrast, the vocal measures did not show a comparable recovery pattern. We therefore interpret the post-exam results as a dissociation between rapidly normalizing subjective affect and a more persistent physiology-linked vocal signal, rather than as evidence that participants remained subjectively distressed.

To address the reviewer’s concern that post-exam responses may depend on performance, we examined whether exam grade was associated with the subjective and acoustic outcomes in the linkable subsample. Because the grade linkage was possible only for a subset of participants (only 62 of 119), these analyses can be only understood as exploratory. As a validity check, the linked grade variable behaved as expected in relation to subjective EMA outcomes. Higher grades were associated with greater post-exam satisfaction, both in the post-on-pre model, median standardized partial slope = 0.367, 89% CrI [0.162, 0.573], pd = .997, and in the change-score model, median = 0.355, 89% CrI [0.112, 0.598], pd = .990. Higher grades were also associated with lower post-exam DASS-stress, most clearly in the post-on-pre model, median = −0.225, 89% CrI [−0.396, −0.054], pd = .981, with a similar but somewhat less directionally certain pattern in the change-score model, median = −0.192, 89% CrI [−0.411, 0.026], pd = .921. Thus, the grade variable followed the theoretically expected variation in subjective post-exam experience.

By contrast, the associations between grade and the vocal measures were uniformly weak and directionally uncertain. Across F0 and NNE, and across baseline, pre-exam, post-exam, stress-change, recovery-change, and total-change indices, the posterior medians were close to zero, with all median correlations below |.10| and pd values ranging only from .502 to .769. The grade-to-recovery models showed the same overall pattern.
